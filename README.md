****Introduction****

 - The two agricultural crops to be discussed in this empirical project are Soybeans and Corn, where the prices of these two goods in relation to each other are analysed using a number of concepts relating to time series analysis. Employing measures, namely cointegration tests and error correction models, while also utilising key components of a time series variable such as unit roots, aided in determining the existence of a causal link between the prices of the two variables.

 - Understanding the nature in which prices of agricultural goods fluctuate and move together, especially in this case, where such trends are found to be cointegrating, is important for supply-demand analysis, as we know from Economic theory that rising prices are a good indicator for increasing levels of consumer demand, and vice-versa. What’s more, this drives the choices firms make at a higher level.

 - **Tests and Econometric Models Utilized**

    - Unit Root
    - Cointegration
    - Engle-Granger Test
    - Johansen Test
  
   **Empirical Results**
 - The first tests ran are informal checks for non-stationarity, a time series plot of the logarithm of corn prices and logarithm of soybean prices and a plot of the first differences of the two variables. The variables both appear to be non-stationary as they do not have a constant mean and variance. As can be seen from (figure 1.1) both corn and soybean prices follow a fairly constant upward trend and exhibit the same cyclic pattern, soybeans are the most volatile of the two variables. This leads us to use differencing to remove trends and generate plots of series of first differences. (Figure 1.2) shows that the mean and variance now appear to be constant implying that the variables are now stationary, again soybeans can be seen to be the more volatile of the two variables.

 - Autocorrelation function plots (ACF) were then generated to further test for non-stationarity. Both corn(figure 2.1) and soybeans(figure 2.2) are ACF plots that show both consistent and significant autocorrelations, additionally both plots drop slowly towards zero which is what we hope for when looking at non-stationary variables. These results provide further evidence of non-stationary among variables. In contrast the ACF plots for the first difference data for both corn(figure 2.3) and soybeans(figure 2.4) only have significant correlations between the 1st and 2nd period, after that all autocorrelations become insignificant and lack consistency. This provides evidence of stationarity for the first difference data.

**Figure 2.1**

![Image](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/15aed77b-fca5-4883-afd7-a23cdad7c79b)

 - Following these informal tests we ran a series of augmented Dickey-Fuller tests (ADF). The null hypothesis in the ADF test is that the data has a unit root. For both corn and soybeans we conducted two tests, one trend type and one drift type. The p-value for all four tests are significant and can be seen in (figure 3.0), this means the results are significant and can be taken into account. For corn “trend” the critical value -3.43 < -2.28 the test statistic. This means we fail to reject the null hypothesis and have unit root and non-stationary variables. The results for each of the three other tests followed this same trend showing non-stationarity and unit roots; a summary of these statistics can be found in (figure 3.0). The findings from all 4 of these tests are coherent with the previous tests run and give further evidence of non-stationarity within the original dataset of both corn and soybean. ADF tests were also run on the first difference data, one on corn_fd “drift” and one on soybeans_fd “drift”. The p-values for each of these tests were also significant and the critical values were greater than the test statistics (figure 3.0), this means we can reject the null hypothesis and there is evidence of stationarity and no unit root.

**Conclusion**

 - The analysis of corn and soybean prices data reveals several significant findings. Initially, examination of time series plots indicates non-stationarity, with both commodities exhibiting stochastic trend behaviour. This observation, supported by consistent upward trajectories and unpredictable spikes, suggests the presence of unit roots in the data. Augmented Dickey-Fuller tests confirm non-stationarity and the existence of unit roots in the original series. Significant p-values across all tests validate the non-stationary nature of the variables. Further, the Engle-Granger test indicates cointegration between corn and soybean prices, implying a long-term linear relationship. This suggests that short-term movements in prices are linked, with both commodities moving towards a long-run equilibrium. Estimation of the ECM delves deeper into the relationship between corn and soybean prices, revealing a significant causal link between the two variables. High values of multiple R-squared and adjusted R-squared indicate that a substantial portion of soybean price variance can be explained by its relationship with corn prices. Overall, the analysis underscores the significant relationship between corn and soybean prices, characterised by cointegration and short-term dynamic adjustments towards long-term equilibrium.
